# New Items Draft: ABC Assessment Expansion (24 to 36 Items)

Date: 2026-03-22
Status: Draft, pending expert review and cognitive pretesting

## 1. Purpose

This document presents 12 new items (2 per subscale) that expand the ABC assessment from 24 to 36 items. The expansion targets two goals: raising subscale reliability from 0.943 to approximately 0.96, and improving per-domain classification agreement from roughly 67% to 75%.

Each subscale gains one forward-scored item (position 5) and one reverse-scored item (position 6). The reverse-scored items bring each subscale to two reverse-coded items (positions 4 and 6), strengthening acquiescence bias detection and addressing the method-factor concern raised by Murphy et al. (2023).

All items use the standard stem: "How often in the past two weeks did you..."

Response scale: 1 = Strongly Disagree, 7 = Strongly Agree.

## 3. The 12 new items

### 3.1 Ambition Satisfaction (AS5, AS6)

**AS5 (forward-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you choose to push yourself in training because you wanted to, not because someone told you to?"

- **Construct:** Autonomy-directed goal pursuit, volitional initiative
- **Rationale:** The existing four items cover goal confidence, progress, path clarity, and realism. None directly captures the volitional quality of ambition, the sense that the athlete drives their own effort. This item targets autonomous initiation, the core of SDT's autonomy construct as it applies to goal pursuit. It complements AS1 (excitement about a goal) by shifting from affect to agency.

**AS6 (reverse-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you feel like your competitive goals no longer mattered to you?"

- **Construct:** Ambition satisfaction (reverse-keyed), disengagement from goal pursuit
- **Rationale:** AS4 already captures a reverse perspective through "realism" (questioning whether goals are realistic). AS6 targets a different facet: the loss of personal significance in competition. Where AS4 checks whether the athlete doubts the path, AS6 checks whether the destination itself has lost meaning. This distinction matters because an athlete can doubt their plan while still caring deeply about the goal, or conversely, have a clear plan for a goal that no longer feels important. Reverse-scoring: high agreement (6 or 7) indicates low ambition satisfaction; recode as 8 minus raw.

---

### 3.2 Ambition Frustration (AF5, AF6)

**AF5 (forward-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did someone in authority make a decision about your athletic future without consulting you?"

- **Construct:** Active thwarting of autonomy-directed goal pursuit by external agents
- **Rationale:** The existing items cover feeling held back (AF1), effort doubt (AF2), external blocks (AF3), and a reverse item about clear path (AF4). AF5 adds a concrete, behaviourally anchored thwarting experience: decisions imposed by coaches, selectors, or administrators. This is not low satisfaction restated negatively. It describes an identifiable external action (an authority figure making a unilateral decision) that blocks goal pursuit. It strengthens the subscale's capacity to detect active environmental obstruction, consistent with Murphy et al. (2023).

**AF6 (reverse-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you feel that your training environment encouraged you to pursue your own competitive ambitions?"

- **Construct:** Ambition frustration (reverse-keyed), absence of environmental thwarting
- **Rationale:** AF4 captures reverse frustration through "clear path." AF6 targets a different facet: whether the training environment actively supports (rather than blocks) the athlete's self-chosen goals. Low agreement indicates that the environment does not support autonomous goal pursuit, which is the frustration pole. This item provides a positive-keyed anchor within the frustration subscale, breaking the all-negative wording pattern that Murphy et al. (2023) identified as a method artifact risk. Reverse-scoring: high agreement (6 or 7) indicates low frustration; recode as 8 minus raw.

### 3.3 Belonging Satisfaction (BS5, BS6)

**BS5 (forward-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did a teammate or coach show they cared about you as a person, not just as an athlete?"

- **Construct:** Relational connection, unconditional regard in the sport context
- **Rationale:** The existing items cover connection (BS1), support and understanding (BS2), authenticity (BS3), and a reverse item about feeling like an outsider (BS4). BS5 adds unconditional relational regard, the sense of being valued beyond one's performance. This is distinct from BS2 (feeling supported) because support can be conditional on performance. BS5 specifically targets the "whole person" dimension of belonging, which SDT research links to sustained engagement (Deci and Ryan, 2000). The item is behaviourally grounded: it asks about observable actions by specific people (teammates or coaches), not abstract feelings.

**BS6 (reverse-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you feel that you could only be close to your teammates when things were going well in your sport?"

- **Construct:** Belonging satisfaction (reverse-keyed), conditional closeness
- **Rationale:** BS4 captures reverse belonging through feeling like an outsider. BS6 targets a subtler facet: the conditionality of closeness. An athlete may not feel excluded (BS4 is fine) yet still sense that relational warmth depends on performance or results. High agreement indicates that belonging is contingent rather than stable, reducing satisfaction. This item captures a pattern common in high-performance environments where relationships deepen during winning streaks and thin during losing ones. Reverse-scoring: high agreement (6 or 7) indicates low belonging satisfaction; recode as 8 minus raw.

---

### 3.4 Belonging Frustration (BF5, BF6)

**BF5 (forward-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did teammates or coaches deliberately leave you out of conversations or decisions that affected you?"

- **Construct:** Active relational exclusion by identifiable agents in the sport environment
- **Rationale:** The existing items cover exclusion (BF1), lack of acceptance (BF2), conditional relationships (BF3), and a reverse item about full acceptance (BF4). BF5 adds a behaviourally specific thwarting experience: deliberate exclusion from conversations or decisions. This is not "I don't feel included" (which could be low satisfaction). It describes an observable action by specific people that blocks belonging. The word "deliberately" anchors the item in active thwarting rather than passive neglect, consistent with Murphy et al. (2023). The item names the agents (teammates or coaches), making the external source of frustration concrete.

**BF6 (reverse-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you feel that your teammates accepted you even when you made mistakes or underperformed?"

- **Construct:** Belonging frustration (reverse-keyed), unconditional acceptance
- **Rationale:** BF4 captures reverse frustration through "full acceptance." BF6 targets a specific test of that acceptance: whether it survives poor performance. This matters in sport because belonging is most vulnerable after mistakes, losses, or visible failure. An athlete might feel generally accepted (BF4 is fine) but experience withdrawal of acceptance after errors. Low agreement on BF6 indicates that belonging is contingent on performance, a form of conditional regard that SDT identifies as a frustration source (Bartholomew et al., 2011). Reverse-scoring: high agreement (6 or 7) indicates low frustration; recode as 8 minus raw.

---

### 3.5 Craft Satisfaction (CS5, CS6)

**CS5 (forward-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you notice yourself getting better at a specific skill through your own effort?"

- **Construct:** Perceived competence growth, mastery through effort
- **Rationale:** The existing items cover competence (CS1), learning (CS2), challenge-readiness (CS3), and a reverse item about feeling out of depth (CS4). CS5 adds perceived improvement over time, the experience of skill trajectory rather than skill level. CS1 captures current competence; CS5 captures the direction of change. An athlete can feel competent today (CS1 is high) without feeling that they are improving (CS5 is low), or vice versa. The phrase "through your own effort" anchors the item in autonomous skill development, distinguishing Craft (mastery-directed) from Ambition (goal-directed). This item also resists cultural bias because effort-based improvement is recognised across sporting traditions.

**CS6 (reverse-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did you feel stuck at your current skill level, unable to progress?"

- **Construct:** Craft satisfaction (reverse-keyed), stagnation
- **Rationale:** CS4 captures reverse craft satisfaction through feeling out of depth. CS6 targets the opposite end of skill mismatch: feeling stuck rather than overwhelmed. An athlete who endorses CS6 is not claiming the environment blocked them (that is craft frustration). They are reporting a subjective sense of plateau, a failure to experience growth. This is a satisfaction deficit, not an external obstruction. The distinction matters for the Murphy et al. (2023) framework: stagnation is about the internal experience of competence, while frustration items must describe external thwarting. Reverse-scoring: high agreement (6 or 7) indicates low craft satisfaction; recode as 8 minus raw.

---

### 3.6 Craft Frustration (CF5, CF6)

**CF5 (forward-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did your training environment focus more on evaluating your performance than on helping you develop?"

- **Construct:** Active thwarting of competence by an evaluative rather than developmental environment
- **Rationale:** The existing items cover being judged rather than developed (CF1), lack of opportunity (CF2), punishment for mistakes (CF3), and a reverse item about a supportive environment (CF4). CF5 adds a systemic thwarting experience: an environment that prioritises evaluation over development. This extends CF1 (individual judgment) to the structural level. The athlete is not saying "I feel incompetent" (low satisfaction). They are saying "the system is set up to rank me rather than grow me." This describes an environmental feature that obstructs competence, consistent with Murphy et al. (2023). It also captures an experience that SDT research identifies as particularly damaging: ego-involving climates that undermine intrinsic motivation (Deci and Ryan, 2000).

**CF6 (reverse-scored)**
"How often in the past two weeks did your coaches create opportunities for you to practise and improve at your own pace?"

- **Construct:** Craft frustration (reverse-keyed), presence of developmental support
- **Rationale:** CF4 captures reverse frustration through a "supportive environment." CF6 targets a specific dimension: whether coaches actively create development opportunities rather than merely avoiding punishment. Low agreement indicates that the coaching environment does not facilitate skill development, which is active competence thwarting by omission of a duty of care. The phrase "at your own pace" anchors the item in autonomy-supportive coaching, linking Craft frustration to the autonomy dimension that SDT predicts moderates the competence-wellbeing relationship. Reverse-scoring: high agreement (6 or 7) indicates low frustration; recode as 8 minus raw.

## 5. Addressing the Murphy et al. (2023) method artifact concern

Murphy et al. (2023) demonstrated that the BPNSFS's six-factor structure may partly reflect a method artifact: satisfaction items are all positively worded and frustration items are all negatively worded, so the satisfaction-frustration distinction could be confounded with keying direction. Their recommendation is clear: mix positive and negative keying within each subscale so that the factor structure cannot be explained by item polarity alone.

The ABC instrument addresses this concern through three design choices.

**Mixed keying within every subscale.** After the expansion, each subscale contains four forward-scored items (positions 1, 2, 3, 5) and two reverse-scored items (positions 4, 6). Neither satisfaction nor frustration subscales are uniformly keyed. A satisfaction subscale contains items where disagreement indicates low satisfaction (forward items) and items where agreement indicates low satisfaction (reverse items). The same logic applies to frustration subscales. If a method factor explains the satisfaction-frustration split, it cannot do so through keying direction alone, because both directions appear in both subscale types.

**Frustration items describe active thwarting, not restated low satisfaction.** Murphy et al. (2023) noted that some BPNSFS frustration items read like the absence of satisfaction rather than the presence of obstruction. The ABC frustration items were written to describe identifiable external actions or environmental features:

- AF5 names an authority figure making unilateral decisions.
- BF5 names teammates or coaches deliberately excluding the athlete.
- CF5 names an environment structured around evaluation rather than development.

Each item identifies who or what is doing the thwarting. None can be rephrased as "I do not feel satisfied" without losing meaning. This ensures that the frustration factor, if it holds in empirical data, reflects a real psychological process (need thwarting) rather than a statistical artifact of negative wording.

**Reverse-scored frustration items describe the absence of thwarting, not the presence of satisfaction.** AF6, BF6, and CF6 are reverse-scored items within frustration subscales. They describe environmental conditions that would reduce frustration (encouragement of autonomous goals, acceptance after mistakes, coaching that creates development opportunities). These are not satisfaction items mislabelled. They ask about the environment's posture toward the athlete's needs, not the athlete's internal experience of fulfilment. An athlete can endorse CF6 ("my coaches create opportunities to improve") while still reporting low craft satisfaction if they feel stuck despite those opportunities (CS6).

**Empirical testability.** The expanded instrument's mixed-keying structure allows the bifactor-ESEM infrastructure (built in Phase 1, Phase 3) to test directly for a method factor. If a keying-direction factor emerges and accounts for substantial variance, the instrument has a method problem. If the six substantive factors hold after modelling the method factor, the satisfaction-frustration distinction is defensible. The infrastructure to run this test is already built. What it needs is empirical data.

## 6. Sport-specificity and cultural accessibility

### 6.1 Sport-specificity

Every item is anchored in the athletic context through at least one of these features:

- **Named agents:** coaches, teammates, selectors, authority figures in sport (AF5, BF5, BS5, CF5, CF6)
- **Named settings:** training environment, competitive context, sport (AS5, AF6, BS6, CF5, CF6)
- **Named activities:** training, practising, competing, developing specific skills (AS5, CS5, CS6)

No item uses generic workplace or school language. An office worker could not complete these items without translation. This is deliberate: the instrument is designed for athletes, and face validity depends on items that feel like they describe the athlete's actual experience.

At the same time, no item names a specific sport, position, or competitive structure. "Training environment" applies to a Division I swimmer, a club rugby player, and an Olympic weightlifter. "Teammates" applies to team and individual sport athletes (training partners serve the same relational function in individual sports). "A specific skill" applies across all sport types without assuming what the skill is.

### 6.2 Cultural accessibility

The items were written with three cultural accessibility principles.

**Simple vocabulary.** No item uses jargon, idiom, or metaphor. The most complex words are "authority," "consulting," "deliberately," "evaluating," and "competitive." All are concrete and translatable. Reading level is approximately Grade 7 (US) or Year 8 (UK), accessible to adolescent athletes and non-native English speakers.

**Concrete behaviours over abstract states.** Where possible, items describe observable events rather than internal states. "Did someone in authority make a decision without consulting you?" is easier to answer than "Did you feel your autonomy was undermined?" because the respondent does not need to know what autonomy means. "Did a teammate show they cared about you as a person?" is easier to answer than "Did you experience unconditional positive regard?" because it asks about a behaviour, not a concept. This concreteness reduces interpretation variance across cultural backgrounds.

**No assumptions about sport structure.** The items do not assume the athlete has a single head coach, plays on a named team, competes in a league, or follows a Western training periodisation model. "Someone in authority" covers coaches, managers, selectors, parents, or club officials. "Training environment" covers structured programmes and informal settings. "Competitive goals" covers podium finishes and personal bests. This generality allows the instrument to function across sporting cultures without modification.

### 6.3 Items flagged for sensitivity review

Three items warrant particular attention during cognitive pretesting:

1. **BF5** ("deliberately leave you out"). The word "deliberately" implies intent, which some athletes may find difficult to judge. Cognitive pretesting should confirm that athletes interpret this as "it felt intentional" rather than "I have proof it was planned." If athletes struggle with this distinction, the item could be revised to "leave you out of conversations or decisions that affected you" without "deliberately."

2. **BS6** ("only be close to your teammates when things were going well"). Athletes in individual sports may interpret "things were going well" differently (personal performance vs. team results). Pretesting should confirm that athletes across sport types apply this to their own competitive context.

3. **CF6** ("at your own pace"). In cultures where coach authority is absolute and athlete autonomy in training is rare, this item may produce floor effects not because the environment is thwarting but because athlete-directed pacing is not expected. DIF analysis across cultural groups should examine this item specifically.

## 7. Reverse-coding summary for the expanded instrument

After the expansion, the reverse-coded items per subscale are:

| Subscale | Reverse-coded items | Total items | Forward items |
|----------|---------------------|-------------|---------------|
| A-Sat | AS4, AS6 | 6 | AS1, AS2, AS3, AS5 |
| A-Frust | AF4, AF6 | 6 | AF1, AF2, AF3, AF5 |
| B-Sat | BS4, BS6 | 6 | BS1, BS2, BS3, BS5 |
| B-Frust | BF4, BF6 | 6 | BF1, BF2, BF3, BF5 |
| C-Sat | CS4, CS6 | 6 | CS1, CS2, CS3, CS5 |
| C-Frust | CF4, CF6 | 6 | CF1, CF2, CF3, CF5 |

Reverse-scoring formula: recoded score = 8 - raw score.

This gives each subscale a 4:2 forward-to-reverse ratio, sufficient to detect acquiescence bias while keeping the majority of items in the natural keying direction for interpretability.

## 8. Next steps

1. **Expert review.** At least one sport psychologist or SDT researcher reviews each item for construct fidelity, clarity, and potential bias.
2. **Cognitive pretesting.** Administer the 12 new items (embedded in the full 36-item set) to 5 to 10 athletes from diverse sports, levels, and cultural backgrounds. Record think-aloud protocols to identify misinterpretation.
3. **Pilot IRT calibration.** Administer the full 36-item instrument to N >= 100 athletes. Estimate GRM parameters for each new item. Accept items with discrimination > 0.80.
4. **Update scoring infrastructure.** Revise `config/irt_parameters.yaml`, `FACTOR_ITEM_INDICES` in `irt_subscale_scoring.py`, and `TIER_DEFINITIONS` in `tier_reliability.py` to reflect the 6-item subscale structure.
5. **Re-run validation suite.** Confirm all existing tests pass with the expanded item set. Run CFA and bifactor-ESEM on pilot data to test the six-factor structure and check for method-factor artifacts.
