# Coach Circumplex Items Draft

Date: 2026-04-20
Status: Draft, pending expert review and cognitive pretesting
Source plan: improvement-plan-personalization-engine.md Section 16.3

## 1. Purpose

This document presents 24 items measuring the need-supportive and need-thwarting climate a coach creates for their athletes. The items sit in a separate instrument, the Coach Circumplex, administered twice: once to the coach as a self-assessment and once to the athletes on the coach's roster as a coach-behavior rating.

The design draws on the empirically derived circumplex of coaching approaches (Aelterman et al., 2019, via Chapter 11 of the Oxford Handbook of SDT) and the autonomy-supportive behavior catalog in Chapter 25. The five facets below are a practical grouping of the eight circumplex approaches into three need-supportive and two need-thwarting styles, sufficient for a 24-item instrument while preserving the autonomy-support, structure, and relatedness-support distinctions that Chapter 45 warns against collapsing.

These items are original to Ero. No licensed scale content is reproduced.

## 2. Design constraints

- **Dual-rater:** Both the coach and the coach's athletes complete the same 24 items. The athlete responses are aggregated to a team mean. The gap between the coach's self-rating and the athlete mean is a diagnostic of its own.
- **Cadence:** Quarterly for coach self-rating; season-start and season-end for athlete ratings. Less frequent than the core ABC cadence because coaching climate moves slowly.
- **Stem for coach self-rating:** "In the past month, how often did you..."
- **Stem for athlete rating of coach:** "In the past month, how often did your coach..."
- **Response scale:** 1 = Never, 4 = Sometimes, 7 = Very Often. Matches the core bank for consistency in respondent experience.
- **No reverse-scored items within facets:** Each item measures one facet directly. The need-thwarting facets are not reverse-scored versions of the supportive facets; they measure distinct behaviors.

## 3. The 24 items

### 3.1 Autonomy-support (CXA1 to CXA5)

The autonomy-support facet captures behaviors that create space for athlete initiative, offer meaningful choice, and convey the reasoning behind requests.

**CXA1**
Coach self: "...acknowledge that an athlete saw a situation differently than you did?"
Athlete rating: "...acknowledge that you saw a situation differently than they did?"

- **Construct:** Perspective-taking, a core autonomy-supportive behavior.

**CXA2**
Coach self: "...offer athletes a genuine choice within a training decision?"
Athlete rating: "...offer you a genuine choice within a training decision?"

- **Construct:** Meaningful choice provision.

**CXA3**
Coach self: "...explain the reason behind a drill or a rule?"
Athlete rating: "...explain the reason behind a drill or a rule?"

- **Construct:** Rationale-giving. Chapter 25 identifies rationales as the highest-leverage autonomy-supportive behavior across domains.

**CXA4**
Coach self: "...ask an athlete for their opinion before deciding?"
Athlete rating: "...ask for your opinion before deciding?"

- **Construct:** Input-seeking. Distinct from choice provision because it precedes the decision.

**CXA5**
Coach self: "...let an athlete take initiative without stepping in?"
Athlete rating: "...let you take initiative without stepping in?"

- **Construct:** Non-interference. Captures the restraint side of autonomy-support that simple "choice" items miss.

### 3.2 Structure (CXS1 to CXS5)

The structure facet captures behaviors that make expectations clear, deliver competence-supportive feedback, and build skill progression. Structure and autonomy-support are empirically distinct (Aelterman et al., 2019): a coach can provide high structure with low autonomy-support, producing rigid-but-clear environments.

**CXS1**
Coach self: "...make the goal of a drill or session clear before starting?"
Athlete rating: "...make the goal of a drill or session clear before starting?"

- **Construct:** Goal clarity.

**CXS2**
Coach self: "...give an athlete specific, constructive feedback on their performance?"
Athlete rating: "...give you specific, constructive feedback on your performance?"

- **Construct:** Competence-supportive feedback. Specificity is the marker.

**CXS3**
Coach self: "...break a difficult skill into steps an athlete could work through?"
Athlete rating: "...break a difficult skill into steps you could work through?"

- **Construct:** Skill scaffolding.

**CXS4**
Coach self: "...follow through on something you said you would do?"
Athlete rating: "...follow through on something they said they would do?"

- **Construct:** Reliability, a structure facet that athletes read as trustworthiness.

**CXS5**
Coach self: "...adjust a plan when it was not working, rather than push through?"
Athlete rating: "...adjust a plan when it was not working, rather than push through?"

- **Construct:** Responsive structure. Distinct from chaos (no structure); captures structure that adapts.

### 3.3 Relatedness-support (CXR1 to CXR5)

The relatedness-support facet captures warmth, interest in the athlete as a person, and demonstration that the coach cares beyond sport outcomes. Chapter 45 notes that autonomy-support scales frequently confound this with autonomy. The items here target relatedness specifically.

**CXR1**
Coach self: "...ask an athlete about something in their life outside sport?"
Athlete rating: "...ask you about something in your life outside sport?"

- **Construct:** Interest in the whole person.

**CXR2**
Coach self: "...show genuine warmth when an athlete was struggling?"
Athlete rating: "...show genuine warmth when you were struggling?"

- **Construct:** Warmth under adversity.

**CXR3**
Coach self: "...remember something specific an athlete told you before?"
Athlete rating: "...remember something specific you told them before?"

- **Construct:** Attunement to the athlete as an individual.

**CXR4**
Coach self: "...celebrate an athlete's non-performance win (academic, personal, social)?"
Athlete rating: "...celebrate your non-performance win (academic, personal, social)?"

- **Construct:** Unconditional regard. Chapter 27 flags conditional regard as a specific need-thwarting pattern.

**CXR5**
Coach self: "...make it clear that an athlete mattered regardless of how they performed?"
Athlete rating: "...make it clear that you mattered regardless of how you performed?"

- **Construct:** Non-contingent mattering. The athlete-side test for unconditional regard.

### 3.4 Controlling (CXC1 to CXC5)

The controlling facet captures pressure tactics, contingent regard, and guilt or shame induction. These are not the inverse of autonomy-support; they coexist with it in some coaches (the "helicopter" pattern Chapter 24 describes in parenting and Chapter 11 adapts to coaching).

**CXC1**
Coach self: "...use guilt or shame to push an athlete to try harder?"
Athlete rating: "...use guilt or shame to push you to try harder?"

- **Construct:** Guilt induction. A defining introjection-producing behavior.

**CXC2**
Coach self: "...withdraw attention or warmth when an athlete performed poorly?"
Athlete rating: "...withdraw attention or warmth when you performed poorly?"

- **Construct:** Conditional regard. Directly maps to Chapter 27's core construct.

**CXC3**
Coach self: "...compare an athlete unfavorably to a teammate or rival to motivate them?"
Athlete rating: "...compare you unfavorably to a teammate or rival to motivate you?"

- **Construct:** Social comparison as pressure.

**CXC4**
Coach self: "...interrupt an athlete to correct them before they finished speaking?"
Athlete rating: "...interrupt you to correct you before you finished speaking?"

- **Construct:** Voice-suppressing behavior. A micro-level controlling pattern.

**CXC5**
Coach self: "...frame rewards as contingent on meeting your expectations rather than as recognition?"
Athlete rating: "...frame rewards as contingent on meeting their expectations rather than as recognition?"

- **Construct:** Controlling use of rewards.

### 3.5 Chaos (CXH1 to CXH4)

The chaos facet captures absence of structure: inconsistency, unpredictability, lack of follow-through. Chaos is empirically distinct from controlling (a chaotic coach is not necessarily controlling, and vice versa).

**CXH1**
Coach self: "...change the plan or expectations without warning athletes?"
Athlete rating: "...change the plan or expectations without warning you?"

- **Construct:** Inconsistency in expectations.

**CXH2**
Coach self: "...forget or drop something you had previously committed to?"
Athlete rating: "...forget or drop something they had previously committed to?"

- **Construct:** Lapses in follow-through. Paired with CXS4 (reliability) to triangulate structure.

**CXH3**
Coach self: "...show up to practice without a clear plan?"
Athlete rating: "...show up to practice without a clear plan?"

- **Construct:** Lack of preparation.

**CXH4**
Coach self: "...respond to the same athlete behavior with different reactions on different days?"
Athlete rating: "...respond to your behavior with different reactions on different days?"

- **Construct:** Inconsistent response. Athletes experience this as disorienting.

## 4. Scoring model

Each facet is a mean of its items on the 1-7 scale, normalized to 0-10:

```
facet_score = ((mean(items) - 1) / 6) * 10
```

The five facets produce two composites:

```
need_supportive = mean(autonomy_support, structure, relatedness_support)
need_thwarting  = mean(controlling, chaos)
```

Dominant-approach classification on the composites:

| need_supportive | need_thwarting | Dominant approach | Coach development framing |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| >= 6.0 | < 4.0 | `supportive` | Continue and refine |
| >= 6.0 | >= 4.0 | `mixed` | Supportive core with need-thwarting episodes to surface |
| < 6.0 | < 4.0 | `under-structured` | Need-support is present but thin; add one supportive facet |
| < 6.0 | >= 4.0 | `thwarting` | Need-thwarting outweighs support; intervention warranted |

## 5. Coach-versus-athlete gap

When both coach self-rating and an athlete aggregate exist for the same coach, compute the gap per facet:

```
gap = coach_self_score - athlete_team_mean
```

| Gap band | Interpretation |
|----------|----------------|
| gap > 2.0 | Coach over-estimates support or under-estimates thwarting |
| -2.0 <= gap <= 2.0 | Self and athlete perceptions aligned |
| gap < -2.0 | Coach under-estimates support or over-estimates thwarting |

On supportive facets, a large positive gap is a flag: the coach believes they are providing more support than athletes experience. On thwarting facets, a large positive gap is also a flag: the coach under-reports pressure or chaos that athletes do experience.

## 6. Evidence gates

Consistent with improvement-plan-personalization-engine.md Section 17.5:

| Condition | Gate | Effect |
|-----------|------|--------|
| At least 3 of a facet's items answered | Passes display gate for that facet | Score shown with hedged language |
| All items in a facet answered | Can pass recommendation gate | Full-confidence coach-facing text |
| Athlete aggregate has fewer than 3 raters | Gap suppressed | Coach self-rating shown alone with a note |
| Either composite built from fewer than 2 of 3 supportive facets | Composite suppressed | Facet scores shown; dominant-approach label withheld |

## 7. Empirical calibration open items

- Subscale reliability (omega) per facet at n >= 50 coaches and n >= 5 athletes per coach.
- Empirical cutpoints for the dominant-approach classification against season-end outcomes (coach retention, athlete engagement, burnout rates).
- Convergent validity against a validated coaching-climate measure (TASQ, PCQ-S, or equivalent, licensing permitting).
- Gap-band thresholds (2.0 points) are theoretical priors; recalibrate against paired coach-athlete data when available.
